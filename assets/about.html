About the telescopes

SALSA (Such A Lovely Small Antenna) is a set of small radio telescopes located at the Onsala Space Observatory on the west coast of Sweden, operated by Chalmers University of Technology. They are available free of charge to anyone who wants to explore radio astronomy.

The telescopes are parabolic dish antennas about 2.3 metres in diameter, equipped with receivers tuned to the 21 cm wavelength (around 1420 MHz). This frequency corresponds to the hyperfine transition of neutral hydrogen, making the telescopes well suited for mapping the Milky Way and studying the large-scale structure of our Galaxy.

Each telescope can be pointed at any position above the local horizon, and can track targets as they move across the sky. The system is fully remote-controlled through this web interface — no special software or hardware is required on your end.

What can I observe?

  • Neutral hydrogen in the Milky Way — map the Galaxy's spiral arms and measure its rotation curve
  • The Sun — measure the antenna beam pattern and study solar radio emission
  • GNSS satellites — detect signals from GPS, Galileo, GLONASS, and BeiDou
  • Any position on the sky above the horizon, in galactic, equatorial, or horizontal coordinates

Location

The telescopes are located at Onsala Space Observatory, coordinates 57°24'N, 11°55'E, at an elevation of approximately 20 m above sea level. Targets with declination above −33° can be observed, and targets above +33° are circumpolar (never set below the horizon).
